Approximating the Future

Jonah Williams

In an era of big and messy data, traditional statistics cannot give us the answers we need. This is not an inner-disciplinary fight between frequentists and bayesians, nor is it a condemnation of the p-value or statistical significance. The truth of the matter is that the focus of academic statistics is different than the needs of business analysis; Where the former is concerned with theory and proof, the latter should be concerned with making practical models to forecast, explain, and discover. If you are given a model with $p < 0.05$, then it's predictions must be fairly accurate, right?

Staistical Significance doesn't mean what you've been led to believe. The common (mis)interpretation goes as follows: If I have $p < 0.05$ then that means I've controlled my false positive rate and my model has less than a 5% chance of being incorrect. Unfortunately there are enough caveats on the above to make it basically unworkable for the purpose its been used. First, P-values in no way account for false negatives, which can be potentially much worse depending on the context of the model. Second, the actual formulation of a P-value is $P(D \ | \ H)$, the probability of generating data under the hypothesis. Our P-value is really telling us about our data, not the model at all! It depends entirely on how well our residual error conform to a well known distribution, and it is trival to get significant p-values with statistical sleight-of-hand and larger samples. Finally, all along we've been using the idea of "how well does this model fit" to try and get at a deeper question: How good our my predictions?

A low p-values, high R-squared, everything we could hope for. Unforunately in this case we can peak behind the curtain: the correlation is just a result of cumulative series. While in this case it is easy to identify the source of the problem, real data is much messier. Rather than trying to identify and name all the possible situations that can give pathological models, wouldn't it be nice if we had a simple way to protect ourselves?

It is possible to estimate our average out-of-sample error. Using the PAC (Probably Approximately Correct) learning framework, we can bound our error with some probability $1 - \alpha$ (that's the probably part) and some tolerance $\epsilon > 0$ (and the approximate part). The setup is fairly simple; we can simulate the arrival of new data by leaving out a subset of our original data, this is known as the test set. It can be shown that the empirical error on this set approximates the "true" error of our model, plus the bounds determined by the constraints above. As an added bonus, this framework is completely agnostic to the methods used to determine the model. Whether it was a linear regression, decision tree, or SVM we can use the same procedure. The only changes that need to be made are the definitions of risk for the loss function( regression versus classification problem). PAC learning protects us from the mistakes of over-eager analysts and overfit models.
